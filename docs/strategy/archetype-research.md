# Cornr — Behavioural Portraits of Seven UK Home-Style Archetypes

*Anthropological field notes for a production writing session. UK first-time buyers, age ~28–38, limited budget, first unrenovated space. Raw material, not finished copy. Specificity beats taste summary throughout.*

---

## 1. The Curator · Mid-Century Modern

### Behavioural tells
- Has a saved search on The Modern House for "under £500k South East London" and refreshes it on the Overground despite not actively looking — checks it the way other people check Strava.
- Walks ten minutes out of their way to photograph the Alexandra Road Estate, Golden Lane, or Span estate railings; the photo lives in a Notes-app folder called "flat ref" and never gets posted.
- Rearranges three objects on the sideboard (ceramic vessel, stacked Cereals/Apartamento, single stem in a Hasami vase) roughly every time they host, shoots it on a 35mm app from the same sofa corner, deletes by Monday.
- Sands and re-oils the Ercol arm with Osmo Polyx on a Sunday morning; posts the before/after on their private Close Friends Story only.
- Goes to the Dulwich Midcentury Modern Fair or the Peckham Car Boot with a specific brief ("small teak side table, under £80, elm if poss") and comes home with a West German pottery vase instead.
- Bookmarks Narchie and Vinterior listings, screenshots them to their partner with "thoughts?", watches the listing for six weeks before it sells to someone else.
- Leaves one wall deliberately unfurnished "for the right thing" — stays empty for eighteen months.
- Refills the Aesop Resurrection hand wash with Dr Bronner's once the bottle is empty.

### Artefact grammar
**Surfaces:** a single well-loved *Kinfolk* or *Apartamento* on the coffee table — not a stack; never a fanned-out *Monocle*. Hasami Porcelain mug; Aesop hand wash by the sink; a Falcon enamel pie dish used as a fruit bowl; a Kinto carafe. An Earl of East or Haeckels candle (Margate credentials matter).

**Walls:** landlord-white, or a careful Farrow & Ball Shaded White / Setting Plaster in one room only. One framed Anni Albers or Josef Frank print from the Tate shop; an unframed Wim Crouwel poster Blu-Tacked at the corners; a small signed ceramic from a maker they met at a fair. No gallery wall.

**Furniture (real inventory):** a vintage Ercol 355 studio couch or 203 Windsor off Vinterior (£400–£900 refurbished) or won on Gumtree collection-only for £75. An IKEA Söderhamn chaise in Viarp beige as the actual sofa, chrome legs swapped for wooden ones off Etsy. A HAY AAC 22 bought in the twentytwentyone sale — never a real Eames DSW. A G Plan Astro or Nathan Circles sideboard from a Bristol seller. A single real Ercol dining chair they paid too much for, plus three non-matching elm chairs found one by one. String shelving or IKEA Ivar stained dark.

**Lighting:** a HAY PAO or Anglepoise Type 75 (not the real Louis Poulsen PH5 — pinned to a "one day" board). An IKEA Regolit that is "basically a Noguchi".

**Kitchen:** landlord white-gloss painted Little Greene Pleat; wooden boards on display; a Sabatier, a Moka pot, sourdough starter in a recycled Bonne Maman jar. Ottolenghi *Simple* and a Claudia Roden on the shelf.

**Conspicuous absences:** no live-edge anything. No DFS, no La Redoute marble-effect coffee table (or if there is one, it is apologised for). No fake plants. No chevron cushions. No surname wall decal. No Dunelm. No rose-gold anything. No mirrored furniture. No grey velvet button-back.

### Language patterns
**They say:** "we went for…", "it's a bit of a project", "it's not perfect but…", "we're living with it for now", "the bones are good", "we'll do it properly eventually", "I got it for nothing off Marketplace", "it's an Ercol, actually", "the flat's basically a Zone 3 Barbican."

**They avoid:** "accent wall", "pop of colour", "statement piece" (ironic only), "cosy" (too Nester), "curated" about themselves (embarrassing), "shabby chic", "investment piece", "Mad Men vibe", "inspo".

**They apologise for:** the magnolia ceiling, the IKEA Malm still in the bedroom, the Söderhamn ("until we save for a Carl Hansen"), the textured-glass bathroom, the lost Made.com sofa voucher from November 2022, the artex they haven't skimmed.

**They defend:** the one Ercol ("it'll outlive us"), the £180 HAY tray, having only two dining chairs, the empty wall.

**They get excited about:** a Modern House listing going up in their postcode, the Barbican Conservatory, a blue-label Ercol in Faversham for £60, a new issue of *Cereal*, the Skandium sample sale, a mews open house in Kensal.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge:** £600–£1,200 on a single signature — a restored Ercol 355 day bed from Vinterior, a G Plan Astro or Nathan Circles, or, if pooling budgets with a partner, two real Carl Hansen CH24 Wishbones at ~£525 each (and sitting on mismatched Ercols for the rest).

**Defer:** the sofa (Söderhamn with Bemz covers "eventually"); proper curtains (pinned bedsheets for 14 months); the rug (IKEA Morum placeholder); the dining table (IKEA Lisabo).

**Regret:** the La Redoute or Made marble-effect coffee table, now described as "a bit Zara Home". Losing a £700 Made order in October 2022. Painting a feature wall Hague Blue and immediately hating it. The £150 Amazon faux-Wishbone whose synthetic cord has gone grey.

**Decade-keep:** the vintage Ercol, the G Plan, an Anglepoise, one Hasami plate set, an inherited Nathan bookcase, a single Carl Hansen chair if they got there.

**Core tension:** they read Dezeen at lunch and live in a 68m² Taylor Wimpey in Walthamstow with combi-boiler bulkheads, a Howdens kitchen they can't replace, and textured ceilings. The mid-century vocabulary is a coping mechanism — a way of saying "I chose this" about a flat they couldn't really choose.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Curator vs Minimalist** — both restrained, but the Curator *owns objects with provenance and will tell you about them at length*. Curator's Ercol is displayed with its age marks and explained (Lucian Ercolani, elm not beech, blue-label 1963); the Minimalist would have replaced it with a soaped-oak bench from Another Country. Curator's shelves: books spines-out. Minimalist's: books turned backwards or none. Curator has visible brass, patinated wood, dust in the grain. Minimalist has matte ceramic, soaped oak, no dust.

**Curator vs Urbanist** — both design-literate, but the Curator is *reverential and historical*; the Urbanist is *directional and present-tense*. Curator quotes The Modern House journal; Urbanist quotes the Wallpaper\* Design Awards 2026. Curator buys second-hand Ercol in natural elm; Urbanist buys new Gubi Beetle in bouclé. Curator's palette is oiled wood, paper, wool, limewash, ochre, olive. Urbanist's is lacquer, chrome, ox-blood, chartreuse. Curator apologises for the Söderhamn; Urbanist chose it in a colour on purpose.

### Sensory anchors
The grain of oiled elm, slightly rough at the arm where a decade of hands have worn through the varnish. Paper-cord on a Wishbone-dupe starting to sag at the middle seat. The nubble of a wool Armadillo rug. The cold smooth shoulder of a Hasami jug. Morning sun comes in low through single-glazed steel-style windows and catches the grain of the G Plan sideboard; dust is visible in the beam and they don't mind. Evenings: warm tungsten from a single pendant — no overhead, ever. An Anglepoise pools a 60-watt circle on a Penguin Classic. The muffled thunk of a Moka pot on an induction ring; a Roberts Revival radio on 6 Music (Mary Anne Hobbs, Sunday mornings); Nick Drake's *Pink Moon* or Khruangbin on a refurbished Rega. Aesop Resurrection at the kitchen sink. Osmo hard-wax oil smell for three days after the Sunday refinish. An Earl of East Greenhouse or Haeckels Dungeness lit at 7pm on a Thursday. Faint damp in the hall cupboard they pretend they can't smell.

## 2. The Nester · Coastal

### Behavioural tells
- Unpacks bedding before books. The bed is properly made — oatmeal linen duvet, four pillows — before the kettle is plugged in. Kate Watson-Smyth is scripture here: "I think everything is copable with if your bed is all plump and appetising. The second thing was an army of table lamps."
- Changes every lightbulb on day one from landlord 6500K "mortuary" bulbs to warm 2700K. Three or four table lamps plugged in before a single picture is hung. Has a strong moral position on the Big Light — it's on only to find a spider or hoover.
- Hangs curtains before painting. Lives happily with landlord magnolia for 18 months but cannot tolerate a bare window; unlined linen from Dunelm or H&M Home goes up week one, "proper" ones (Piglet, John Lewis) arrive month six.
- Weekly supermarket flowers on rotation — eucalyptus, then tulips, then stocks — always £4–£6 from Sainsbury's or Waitrose, always in the same stoneware jug (often a holiday souvenir, sometimes her mum's). Has never owned a "proper" vase.
- Cushions and a throw on the sofa on day one even if the sofa is still the old Ektorp from the rental. The throw gets straightened every time she stands up. Nine cushions on the bed.
- Brings cuttings from her mum's garden — a jasmine, a spider-plant baby in a jam jar on the kitchen windowsill — before she has bought a bin.
- Offers visitors tea in a matching mug within four minutes of the doorbell. Apologises for the kitchen ("sorry, we haven't done anything to it yet") before the guest has taken their shoes off.
- **The flat is "done" — to her own standard — within 6 weeks.** The kitchen overhaul is still five years away but the *feeling* is locked in almost immediately. This is the single sharpest behavioural fingerprint against the Curator.

### Artefact grammar
**Present:** a slubby oatmeal linen throw (Piglet in Bed Crinkle Throw at £95 if splurging; H&M Home washed linen if not). A stoneware fruit bowl from Daylesford or Oliver Bonas with three lemons and a stone fruit. A stack of softback cookbooks displayed spine-out — Skye McAlpine *A Table for Friends*, Anna Jones *One*, Nigel Slater *Kitchen Diaries*. Diptyque Figuier in the downstairs loo, or (when skint) the M&S Fig or MUJI Green Fig as a knowing dupe. A Neom Real Luxury in the bedroom. A rattan tray on the coffee table holding two books and a candle. A white or natural linen slipcover sofa — Loaf Squishmeister in Whipped Cream, Sofa.com Teddy, Swoon Althea — often financed on V12. Bleached-wood chopping boards left out as decor. A jute or wool-loop rug (Cox & Cox, Garden Trading). Unlined linen curtains, sometimes puddle-length. A single framed print leaning on the mantelpiece for eight months before it's hung.

**Absent:** bold pattern above a subtle ticking stripe. Black. Chrome. Glass coffee tables. Feature walls. Grey. Anything chevron or with a quote on it. Ceiling spotlights used alone. A reed diffuser in the loo — naff. It's a candle or nothing.

### Language patterns
Core vocabulary: **cosy, homely, calm, lived-in, warm, soft, layered**. Sentences that recur verbatim on Mumsnet and Instagram:
- "I just want it to feel lived in."
- "It's a journey." / "It's slowly coming together."
- "Once I've got a rug in here it'll make such a difference."
- "Sorry about the magnolia — we're going to paint, but it's literally everything else first."
- "It's not Pinterest-perfect but it's ours."
- "The bedroom is my sanctuary."
- "I know I spend too much on candles but…" (defensive, followed by a laugh).

She says *sitting room* or *living room* — never *lounge*, never *my space*, never *my vibe*. Americanisms grate. She describes a paint colour as "a lovely warm off-white" but does know Slipper Satin from Pointing.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge (the decade-keepers):** the sofa — linen-slip, neutral, bought new on finance, defended for ten years ("we'll have this forever"). The bed and mattress. 100% linen bedding from Piglet, Soak & Sleep, The White Company ("it gets softer every wash"). A good wool rug. One proper table lamp with a Pooky or Pinkhouse shade.

**Defer:** art on walls (framed prints lean for two years). A proper dining table — eats on the sofa off a tray, or at a too-small IKEA drop-leaf, for the first three years. The kitchen — lives with buy-to-let white high-gloss and dreams of Neptune Chichester in Shell. Any built-in storage.

**Regret:** the rattan pendant that yellowed. The polyester "linen-look" Amazon cushions that pilled in six months. The cheap jute rug that shed onto the sofa. Anything bought in a rush from H&M Home sale.

**The 6-week rule:** by week six the throw is on the sofa, the bedside lamps are on, the fig candle is burning, there are flowers in a jug — and she genuinely believes the flat is finished, even though the skirting is still landlord-cream and there's no extractor fan. The Curator would be appalled at how fast she has declared victory.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Nester vs Romantic (the critical seam).** Romantic's bedding: Cabbages & Roses florals, a pink gingham valance, a Tess Newall hand-painted scallop, a Matilda Goad ruffled bathmat. **Nester's bedding: plain oatmeal linen, a single ticking-stripe pillowcase, a cream-trimmed drum lampshade.** Romantic mixes three florals in one room and calls it maximalist. **Nester's pattern ceiling is a narrow navy ticking stripe on one cushion.** If she buys a Matilda Goad scallop it's the cream raffia one, never the painted one. Romantic collects brocante and ceramic dogs; Nester collects plain stoneware, wooden bowls, a single Astier de Villatte plate on open shelving. Romantic says "I love a frill"; Nester says "I just want it calm."

**Nester vs Minimalist.** Minimalist's fruit bowl is empty, made of smoked oak, lives in a cupboard. **Nester's fruit bowl has three lemons in it and lives on the counter, always.** Minimalist puts the throw away; Nester drapes it — the throw *is* the point. Minimalist's bed has one pillow each, no decorative cushions; Nester's bed has nine and she re-plumps them every morning. Minimalist conceals; Nester displays, softly. Both rooms read "calm" but the Nester's calm is *upholstered*; the Minimalist's is *edited*.

### Sensory anchors
The grain of washed linen pulled under the chin at 10pm. Morning light through unlined linen curtains onto a wooden floor. The click-and-roar of a kettle (Dualit, or the Sainsbury's one she's saving up to replace). Fig drifting from the downstairs loo. Sourdough toast, proper butter. Coffee from a Moka pot or French press, never instant in company. Radio 4 in the morning (*Today*, then *Woman's Hour*) until 10am, then a Spotify playlist — "Cozy Acoustic Morning", or her own "slow kitchen". A Neom candle lit the moment it gets dark, even in June. The soft *thwump* of a linen-covered cushion being re-plumped. Never: incense, Glade, a loo diffuser, Yankee Candle ("too much").

## 3. The Maker · Industrial

### Behavioural tells
- **At the viewing**, ignores the estate agent's patter about the "recently fitted kitchen" and knocks on the chimney breast, lifts a corner of carpet in the back bedroom to see what's under, asks what year the rear extension went on, photographs the consumer unit.
- Keeps a cardboard box of timber offcuts under the bed or behind the sofa — "I might need that length of 4x2." Refuses to take it to the tip for four years.
- **Owns tools before owning a sofa.** Makita combi-drill, Stanley knife, Bahco handsaw, a set of chisels — all in the flat before the bed-frame arrives. The sofa is a hand-me-down or Facebook Marketplace.
- Saturday morning is Toolstation or the Wickes trade counter — back by 11 with MDF, brass screws and a new 18mm flush-cut saw blade "since I'm here." Will drive to Axminster Tools in Basingstoke on a day off.
- Rips up the carpet in week one. Sands the boards themselves over a weekend — hires the belt sander from HSS — regrets not wearing a better mask. Finishes in Osmo hardwax oil, matt, because kezzabeth.co.uk said to.
- Builds their own shelves from scaffold boards sourced off eBay or a local scaff yard (not pre-cut — that'd be cheating), pairs with black steel brackets off Etsy. Shelves go up before the curtains.
- Facebook Marketplace stalker for a Sjöbergs or vintage joiner's bench, a cast-iron vice, a Singer sewing table base, school lab stools, a Vitsoe 606 ("but nobody ever sells that cheap").
- Relationship with the landlord is combative-tolerant. Politely, in writing, asks if they can "expose a small area of brickwork in the chimney alcove" and paint the front door Farrow & Ball Railings. Does it whether or not they reply.

### Artefact grammar
**Present:** an Anglepoise 1227 (often a genuine vintage one in battered chrome, rewired themselves with textile flex from Dowsing & Reynolds). A cast-iron Le Creuset (wedding present or Bicester Village run); a carbon-steel De Buyer Mineral B, seasoned mottled black. A Blenheim Forge knife or — pre-Blenheim — a Sheffield carbon blade. A Kilner jar of sourdough starter labelled in Sharpie. Scaffold-board shelves on industrial brackets. A Hultafors or Gränsfors Bruks axe on the wall — actually used for kindling. A Carhartt WIP Detroit or Filson waxed tin-cloth coat on a peg salvaged from Retrouvius or Lassco. A Rapha or Castelli cap in the hall; a Brompton or steel-frame tourer against the hallway wall. Terracotta pots, never plastic; Monstera, rubber plant, Pilea cuttings in jam-jars. A V60 or Aeropress; a battered Moka pot. A Melin Tregwynt throw on the armchair (the splurge).

**Absent:** florals, chintz, ditsy prints, anything white-plastic, gloss-white IKEA Malm (they'd have built it from pine and wax). No grey velvet buttoned headboard, no reed diffuser, no decorative-orb tray, no "Live Laugh Love". No Edison bulbs on twisted cloth flex — that's 2016 and they know it.

### Language patterns
Trade-counter, not magazine. **Solid. Proper. Sturdy. Honest. Decent. Had to.** "It's solid oak, not veneer." "I made that." "That's from an old school science lab." "Took the Stanley knife to it." "Got it off a skip in Clapton." "It just needed a sand and a wax." "Works a treat."

Retrouvius and Lassco are pronounced with quiet reverence; a trip to their Harrow Road yard is an event. They get genuinely excited about: a decent secondhand Record No. 5 plane, Axminster doing 20% off, a jigsaw finally powerful enough, a Makita tracksaw they can justify. "Festool's the holy grail but I'm not paying £700 for a saw right now."

They apologise for unfinished things — "sorry about the wood filler, I'll get the sander over it" — and defend the mess of a half-finished project: "that's going to be a bench seat, I've got the timber." They avoid curated, edit, piece, elevated, statement, moment, considered — all too Urbanist/Curator-speak. Never *styling*.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge:** tools first, every time. A Makita LXT combi-drill and impact set. A Festool tracksaw is aspirational — most settle for the Makita SP6000 on a 3m rail (UK Workshop forum threads are full of this exact calculation). A proper bench — Sjöbergs if lucky, self-built from 4x2 if not. F&B Railings on the front door and Down Pipe on kitchen cabinets (bought in 2.5L, cut with Johnstone's on interior walls to stretch). An Anglepoise. A Blenheim Forge knife once savings allow — £300 is "investment, it'll last 40 years."

**Defer:** soft furnishings ("cushions can wait"); the kitchen (lives with Howdens for 18 months, then rips it out themselves — probably a plywood-and-beech Sebastian-Cox-for-deVol knockoff they build in evenings); curtains; a proper dining table (a door on trestles for year one).

**Regret:** nothing they made — even the wonky bits become story. The **flatpack** they caved and bought (often for a partner who wanted it done now) rankles — "I could have built that."

**Decade-keep:** the tools. The cast iron. The Anglepoise. The scaffold shelves. The axe. The carbon-steel pan by now black as a kiln.

**Crucial:** the Maker's flat is **visibly unfinished for 2+ years**. Half a wall stripped to brick because "I'll decide what to do with it." A shelving unit that's been "next weekend" for three Christmases. Skirting off in the hallway. Diagnostic opposite of the Nester, whose flat is finished within 8 weeks.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Maker vs Storyteller.** Both own a 1950s chest of drawers. The Storyteller tells you it was their grandmother's in Pontypridd and they re-lined the drawers with a Liberty scrap. The Maker tells you the drawer runners were knackered, they re-cut them from beech, and the handles are brass cup from Toolstation. Storyteller buys at car boots for **provenance**; Maker buys at reclamation yards for **timber**. Storyteller = who held it. Maker = what it's made of.

**Maker vs Urbanist.** Both can be dark-palette — both might own Down Pipe. Stand in the kitchen: the Urbanist's cabinets are handle-less, flush, finger-pull, sprayed, seamless — construction hidden. The Maker's cabinets are visibly screwed, dovetails on drawer-fronts, worktop is an oiled scaffold-board, pipework exposed on purpose. Urbanist = sharp. Maker = honest about how it's made.

### Sensory anchors
Linseed oil, sawdust (beech is sweet, MDF is acrid — they know the difference), WD-40, V60 coffee, sourdough proving on the radiator, a wet waxed-cotton jacket in the hallway in October. A random-orbit sander on a Saturday 10am. 6 Music (Lauren Laverne, Iggy 4–6). Kettle. Ping of a tape measure retracting. A chop saw in short bursts. The click of bike cleats on bare floorboards. A single warm 2700K bulb on a textile flex hanging low over the table, a battered Anglepoise pool on the bench, long shadows on a brick alcove. Raw sawn timber, the glassy feel of oiled oak, cold forged steel, cable-knit wool (Melin Tregwynt if splurging, charity shop if not), a cast-iron handle always slightly too hot.

## 4. The Minimalist · Japandi

### Behavioural tells
- Post is dealt with at the door, standing up. A shallow tray or lacquer bowl holds keys, AirPods, one unopened letter max. Anything in a Jiffy bag is opened, broken down, and recycled within 90 seconds of entering the flat. There is never a pile by the kettle.
- **The dish rack is empty by bedtime, always.** Clean dishes are towel-dried and returned to the cupboard immediately — leaving a mug to air-dry causes a low, persistent discomfort (the clutter-cortisol feedback loop is real for them).
- Shopping is unpacked, decanted, and packaging leaves the flat before they sit down. Rice into glass or ceramic. The Ocado paper bag folded flat. Plastic is visible for four minutes.
- Shoes off at the threshold; slippers (Muji room sandals or Native & Co) lined up facing outward like a miniature genkan. Guests are gently, apologetically, asked to do the same.
- Cables live in a single Muji polypropylene box, each coiled with a Velcro tie, sometimes labelled in pencil on a paper tab. Spare cables are not kept "just in case."
- **Visitor prep is removal, not addition.** Before people come over they take things off surfaces. A Nester adds cushions; the Minimalist subtracts.
- Gifts they don't want are photographed, thanked for, and quietly rehomed within a fortnight (Kondo-influenced: "thank it for its service"). They will not display it to be polite.
- Tea is made by method — kyusu or Hario, leaves weighed, water off-boil. Instant coffee is a minor moral event.
- Makes the bed within 90 seconds of getting out of it, close to hospital corners. The throw, if there is one, is folded — never piled.

### Artefact grammar
**Present (and usually singular):** a Hasami Porcelain mug in matte grey; one Jars Céramistes or Kaikado tin; a Muji aroma diffuser running hinoki or cedarwood; a Shaker peg rail holding one coat and one tote; pale oak dining table (Muji or made-to-order from Another Country / Wood & Metal / Fell); two to four Carl Hansen CH24 Wishbones in oil-oak, or the cheaper Muji oak chair if still saving; Toast washed-linen-cotton duvet in ecru or undyed; a single Frama, Menu/Audo, or Anglepoise pendant; a single *Kinfolk* Vol. or *Cereal* on the bedside table (one, not stacked — stacking reads as Curator); a Nordic Knots wool rug in oatmeal; linen or washi paper blinds, never curtains. A Japanese knife on a magnetic strip; a single wooden spoon in a ceramic utensil pot.

**Absent:** bright colour; florals; gallery walls; open shelves piled with books; plastic storage tubs (polypropylene OK if Muji and hidden); scented candles with fruit names; Dieter Rams monographs on display (Curator tell); anything "collected"; anything from HomeSense. Wall art: one large-format black-and-white print (Sugimoto-adjacent, often by a friend) — or nothing.

### Language patterns
**They say:** "pared back," "calm," "quiet," "we decluttered," "it feels lighter," "honest materials," "we wanted it to breathe," "I don't see the point of [coffee table book / coaster set / scatter cushion]," "I don't want another [mug / candle / vase], I've got one I love." They say *put away* more than *display*. They apologise pre-emptively — "sorry, it's a bit bare" — especially to parents, who ask "where's all your stuff?" and "have you actually moved in yet?"

**They get excited about:** a trip to Muji Tottenham Court Road (they'll book the Interior Advisor slot), a Toast sample sale, a new *Cereal*, a visit to Native & Co on Redchurch Street, a Francis Gallery show in Bath.

**They avoid:** "cosy" (Nester-coded), "collected" (Storyteller-coded), "statement piece" (Urbanist-coded), "eclectic," "pop of colour," "curated" (Curator, too LinkedIn). They are wary of the word *minimalist* itself — Rosa Park (*Cereal*) publicly pushes back on it; they prefer *pared back* or *quiet*.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge:** one CH24 Wishbone (or a pair, stretched to four over years — £1,035+ each at Heal's or Chaplins); Toast or Piglet linen bedding washed to softness; one pendant from Frama or Menu-Audo; a Japanese knife; the pale-oak dining table. They spend Curator-level money on fewer items.

**Defer:** almost everything. Walls undecorated for two-plus years *on purpose*. The sofa is often the last thing bought because they can't find "the one." A Maruni Hiroshima chair is the ultimate unlocked achievement.

**Regret:** the Muji three-seater that sagged after 18 months; any Black Friday purchase ("I panicked"); Amazon Basics storage baskets bought in the first month that don't match anything; buying a rug before the sofa.

**Decade-keep:** the Wishbone, the Global or Tojiro knife, linen bedding (softens annually), a single Hasami tea set, the Kondo-trained wardrobe system.

**Crucial tension:** the flat reads as under-furnished to visitors. *That is the point.* Their reference is a Kinuta Tokyo studio; their reality is a 70s ex-local-authority flat off Lea Bridge Road or a Salford Quays new-build with paper-thin walls. They resolve the gap by ruthless editing, not renovation.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Minimalist vs Curator (the sharp one).** The Curator's shelf is a stage. The Minimalist's shelf is sometimes empty and *that is finished*. Curator owns ~30 visible objects in the living room; Minimalist owns ~6, at least two functional (diffuser, lamp). Curator *rearranges*; Minimalist *removes*. Curator buys a HAY or USM sideboard to **display** ceramics on; Minimalist buys a Muji or Another Country sideboard to **hide** them inside. Curator's coffee table has three books fanned; Minimalist's has one book, or nothing, and they've considered not owning a coffee table. Curator reads *Kinfolk* and *Wallpaper\**; Minimalist reads *Cereal*, Koren's *Wabi-Sabi*, Sasaki's *Goodbye, Things*. In Daniel Miller's framing: Curator is *householder-as-curator*; Minimalist is *householder-as-designer* — "no objects are allowed that intrude upon the aesthetic."

**Minimalist vs Nester.** Both use oatmeal, linen, wood. But Nester's bed is made with the throw rumpled at the foot and three cushions propped; Minimalist's is flat, throw folded into a drawer. Nester's sofa has four cushions and a visible throw; Minimalist's has one cushion at most, often zero. Nester says "cosy"; Minimalist says "quiet." Nester lives in soft surfaces; Minimalist conceals them until needed.

### Sensory anchors
Shoji-filtered light through a washi or unbleached linen blind — never heavy curtains. The floor is oak, soaped or white-oiled. Scent: hinoki or cedarwood from a Muji diffuser or Hetkinen reed. Candles burn only in evenings, usually unscented or one clean fig/tobacco note. **Default soundtrack is silence** — not Spotify, not background radio. If music, it's ambient at low volume (Nils Frahm, Hania Rani, Haruomi Hosono). The worn handle of a single wooden spoon in the utensil pot; the matte-grain weight of a Hasami mug; the cool bevel of a stone bath mat; linen bedding through 300 washes, warm-air soft. Tea leaves measured, water 80°C, brewed in a kyusu. The ritual is not performative; it's load-bearing for their nervous system. The empty surface is *how they breathe.*

## 5. The Romantic · French Country

### Behavioural tells
- Reshades every cheap lamp base (IKEA Hemma, Habitat charity find) with a pleated, scalloped or gathered shade. Four lamps in a flat have four different patterns — a Matilda Goad raffia scallop on the sideboard (£70–£160 new; one splurge, the rest Etsy/Dunelm dupes), a Pooky pleated Fermoie stripe in the bedroom, a Molly Mahon block-print beside the bath. Apologises for the dupes while pointing out "honestly you can't tell."
- Gets up at 5.30am twice a year for Kempton Park Sunbury (2nd and last Tuesday of the month). Takes cash because "dealers hate card." Texts friends live from a field in Middlesex: "BRASS CANDLESTICKS £8 EACH — HOW MANY DO YOU WANT." Panic-Ubers a brown-furniture chest she can't fit on the Overground.
- Paints the kitchen cabinets in F&B Setting Plaster or Sulking Room Pink over a long weekend — Krud Kutter on Friday night, primer Saturday, two coats of eggshell Sunday. Posts the "before" on an Instagram Story the minute she lifts a roller.
- Fresh flowers from the Saturday market weekly — stocks in April, dahlias from July, tulips in spring, branches from the common in autumn. Dries the hydrangeas in September, sheds them into a chintz jug, leaves them there until February.
- Tablescapes on a Tuesday for nobody. Block-print napkins, mismatched charity-shop glass, a beeswax candle, a single rose in a bud vase per placing. Photographs the empty table before anyone eats.
- Follows @rita_k_shopping, @avwantiques, @benpentreath, DMs antique dealers directly about a green Staffordshire spaniel, a 19th-century oil of a sheep, a piece of brown furniture that won't fit.
- Layers rugs on the landlord carpet: cheap IKEA seagrass base, a bleeding Turkish runner on top. Rotates throws seasonally — linen ticking in summer, fringed wool plaid in October.
- Keeps a physical folder of Colefax & Fowler, Fermoie and House of Hackney swatches on the kitchen table for months before committing.

### Artefact grammar
**Lighting:** Matilda Goad raffia scallop (hero lamp, one real, rest dupes); Pooky pleated in Fermoie or Penny Morrison fabric; an Etsy frilled gathered shade; pairs of brass candlesticks on every surface; a Pooky brass multi-arm pendant or vintage chandelier in the hall.

**Textile:** fringed cushion on the sofa; a Soane or Penny Morrison cushion made from a half-metre remnant; bullion trim on a skirted side table (IKEA Lack under a tablecloth); block-printed napkins; Fermoie lampshade stripe; a ticking-stripe blind behind thin cotton curtains.

**Ceramics & surfaces:** chinoiserie blue-and-white ginger jar from a BHF in Dulwich; dried hydrangeas in a Burleigh chintz jug; a pair of Staffordshire spaniels; mismatched transferware from Kempton; an open biscuit tin kept out on the counter.

**Walls:** one wall of House of Hackney, Morris & Co Willow Bough, or Cole & Son Hummingbirds (sample sale, one roll saved by papering only the chimney breast); a charity-shop oil portrait of somebody else's ancestor; a botanical gallery wall of £6 Kew prints in mismatched gilt.

**Kitchen:** a Gumtree pine dresser painted in F&B Green Smoke or Pea Green; mismatched-but-pretty plates; brass hooks from Corston or YesterHome; a copper jelly mould on display only.

**Books on the coffee table:** Rita Konig; Ben Pentreath *English Decoration*; Pearl Lowe *Faded Glamour*; Beata Heuman *Every Room Should Sing*; Matilda Goad *Imperfect Pretty*; Kit Kemp.

**Absent:** matte black, exposed brick, industrial pendants, concrete, stainless, chrome, open shelving of white crockery, a single bouclé chair used as statement.

### Language patterns
**Positive:** "pretty," "lovely," "proper cosy," "a bit of a find," "gathered," "layered," "frilled," "twee in a good way," "more is more," "can you believe it was £12," "I just think it wants more pattern."

**Defensive:** "I know it sounds like a lot but it all works," "you can't have too many lamps," "walls aren't pink yet, don't judge," "magnolia is going, I promise," "it needs softening."

**Excitement verbs:** *grabbed, nabbed, had to have it, scored.*

**Excited about:** a Kempton Park haul; the House of Hackney sample sale (£2 door, Hackney Foodbank); Matilda Goad drops; Pooky's annual sale; Rita Says newsletter landing; Pentreath's Monday blog; "Colefax has reprinted the old Brooke Stripe."

**Avoids:** *pared back, minimal, restrained, edit, greige, bouclé, statement piece, curated* (used seriously). Note: the archetype herself rejects "grandmillennial" as twee/American — she'd say *English country* or *a bit Rita Konig* or just *pretty*.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge:** a 2-metre Fermoie or Colefax remnant off eBay for curtains (eats beans for a month, saves for a year); one roll of House of Hackney or Morris & Co for a chimney breast; a Pooky brass multi-arm; a real Matilda Goad scallop for the sitting-room lamp.

**Defer:** the kitchen (paints cabinets, swaps handles to brass, keeps the worktop for five years); flooring (layered rugs; sands floorboards, never replaces); the bathroom (sticks with avocado or a cheap respray, hangs a scalloped mirror and a gilt oil above the loo).

**Regret:** wallpaper chosen from a phone screen not a sample — "it's too much, I love it but I can't eat in here"; a woodwormed Kempton chest she treats with Cuprinol and worries about for two years; too-dark a pink (Sulking Room in a north-facing room going "purple").

**Decade-keep:** granny's Royal Doulton; any monogrammed linen; brass candlesticks; the first oil portrait (almost named); a pair of Staffordshire dogs.

**Crucial:** flat reads FINISHED **and BUSY** within a year — every sightline has a pattern, a lamp, a flower, a small object. Coherence is achieved by **theme** (pink/green/burgundy, soft, layered, pretty), not by period. A Victorian dresser sits happily next to a 2024 Matilda Goad rug because both read pretty. This is what distinguishes her from the Storyteller: she edits out objects that don't read pretty even if they have a story.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Romantic vs Nester.** Nester fears pattern past a ticking stripe; Romantic stacks — floral cushion on gingham chair against chintz curtain against striped lampshade, defended as "it all sits in the same colour family." Nester's lampshade: plain drum from John Lewis. Romantic's: scalloped, frilled, pleated, trimmed. Nester paints Skimming Stone, Shaded White, Slipper Satin. Romantic paints Setting Plaster, Sulking Room Pink, Pink Ground, Book Room Red. Nester buys Neptune new, in one go, matching. Romantic buys a chest off Gumtree and a dresser at Kempton and mixes them with a fortnight between. Nester's flowers: one type (white tulips in a glass vase). Romantic's: mixed stems in a chintz jug, dried hydrangeas, a single rose by the bed.

**Romantic vs Storyteller.** Both collected, both love brown furniture. Decisive: the Romantic rejects objects that are ugly-but-meaningful. An inherited 1970s teak sideboard will be charity-shopped or painted Pea Green because teak reads wrong; the Storyteller would keep it for the story. The Romantic organises a shelf by **colour and shape** (blue-and-white together, symmetrical pair of spaniels flanking a jug). The Storyteller organises by **provenance** (grandad's camera next to a Moroccan bowl next to a gig poster). The Romantic's flat photographs as one mood in every room; the Storyteller's is a biography.

### Sensory anchors
Beeswax just blown out. Cut stocks and dahlias, stems still wrapped in brown paper. Dried hydrangeas going papery in a chintz jug. The rustle of thin cotton curtain lined in contrast pink. Diptyque Roses on the mantelpiece, Jo Malone Peony on the hall console, a True Grace Portobello somewhere cheaper in the loo. An open Fortnum's biscuit tin (empty — kept for the tin). Classic FM in the kitchen on a Sunday; Radio 3 in the bath; Nick Cave, Joni Mitchell or Florence on the record player for a dinner. The must of old fabric from a bag of Kempton linens she hasn't washed yet. Afternoon light diffused through unlined cotton, falling across a pink wall.

## 6. The Storyteller · Eclectic Vintage

### Behavioural tells
- **Narrates unprompted.** Hands you a mug and you get its provenance: "That was my nan's, she had it from the '77 Jubilee, drank Mellow Birds out of it every morning until she died." They can do this for any object within arm's reach — cost, context, who held it, what it replaced.
- **Saturday is a market, not a lie-in.** Kempton at 6:30am if serious, Battersea Boot 1:30pm if hungover, Deptford on Fridays before work, Glasgow Barras on a Sunday if north. They know which dealer has the 1950s Bakelite, and they say hello by first name.
- Touches while talking. Fingertips trail over a ceramic rim, a brass candlestick, the chipped edge of a Denby plate as they tell its story. Objects are prompts, not décor.
- Hangs before sensible. A gallery wall of framed postcards, school reports and a nan-letter is up before the sofa is plumb against the wall. A working hall bulb is deferred; the 1920s map of Ceylon goes up first.
- **Refuses to replace old with new.** A lamp breaks; the next six weekends are spent looking for a 1960s brass replacement on Vinterior, eBay, at Greenwich Market — rather than a placeholder IKEA one. The "meantime" is morally loaded.
- Keeps tin archives. A biscuit tin (Huntley & Palmers, Quality Street) per life-era: one of letters, one of postcards, one of ticket stubs. A named paper folder of obituaries clipped from local papers and the *Guardian*.
- Attends house clearances as civic duty. A text from an aunt in Pontypridd — "nan's going into the home, come this weekend" — is non-negotiable. Drives a Zipcar van home full of unsellable things that are now freighted.
- Photographs objects, not rooms. Camera roll is close-ups of glaze crackle, a signature on the back of a print, a Pitlochry charity-shop price sticker — never the "room shot" a Curator would post.

### Artefact grammar
The mantelpiece is the index. A pair of 19th-c. Staffordshire spaniels — one inherited, one found singleton at Sunbury and "paired up" despite wrong size — flank a brass carriage clock that works only occasionally. Between them: a taxidermy magpie under a glass dome, a 1920s samovar, a Toby jug, a framed photograph of someone's great-aunt in WRNS uniform. Pair with a signed Paul Nash print from Abebooks and a William De Morgan tile propped, unhung, on the hearth.

Textiles do the heavy lifting: a Welsh blanket (inherited, Melin Tregwynt or similar, washed soft), a Turkish rug from Deptford or Bazaar in Stoke Newington, a kilim runner fading in a sunny patch. Seating: a hand-me-down Parker Knoll or a Facebook Marketplace Ercol needing re-webbing.

Kitchen: mismatched Denby Arabesque and Portmeirion Botanic Garden (never bought as a set — acquired one plate at a time); a dented Peek Frean's biscuit tin used for flour; a 1970s Bodum Chambord with a replacement-glass history; a flame-orange Le Creuset inherited, chipped on the lid. A Denby teapot. A knife block of mismatched bone-handled cutlery.

Walls: a chronology gallery wall — wedding photographs next to a 1920s map of Ceylon, next to a framed school tie, next to a Blue Peter badge in a clip frame, next to a Cecil Beaton postcard from the NPG shop. **No visual logic, pure meaning-density.**

Books are infrastructure. Penguin orange-spine Modern Classics piled sideways on every horizontal; Virago greens; Faber poetry; face-down hardbacks on the arm of the sofa. Hay-on-Wye and Scarthin Books receipts tucked inside front covers.

**Absent:** matching anything, flat-pack, new-from-this-year. Any new thing is a forced replacement — mattress, fridge, extension lead — and is mildly apologised for.

### Language patterns
Possessive-genealogical. "This was my nan's." "I got this from Sue Ryder in Harrogate." "There's a story here." "It was a fiver at Deptford — I couldn't not." "It reminded me of my dad." "I've had this since I was about nineteen." "It came from a house clearance in Pontypridd." Provenance clauses stack; single objects acquire three or four.

Defensive register around mess: clutter is **character**, mess is *evidence of life*, dust is *patina*. They apologise for **nothing** except the TV remote (always buried under a Saturday *Review*). They will **not** apologise for the kitchen.

Excited nouns: *skip find, house clearance, Abebooks, Oxfam Books, Hay-on-Wye, Scarthin, Cecil Court, Sue Ryder in Harrogate / Ilkley / Sevenoaks, Alfies, Camden Passage, Sunbury, Ardingly, Golborne Road, Barras.*

Banned words (they wince): **edit, curated, pared back, minimal, matching set, statement piece, scheme.** "Curated" is a tell of the Curator archetype and they feel the distance. Luke Edward Hall's phrasing is close: "I'm more attached to little things that have stories… that connection to things I've done, people I've met, places I've been." They quote books in conversation — a Larkin line, something from Penelope Fitzgerald, a bit of Alan Bennett — without flagging it as a quote.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge.** Almost never on something new. The single annual splurge is a provenance piece: a Troika wheel vase, a signed first edition (Plath, Berger, Angela Carter), a Turkish rug from Bazaar in Stoke Newington or a Deptford dealer whose name they know. Justified entirely by story — "it's an Oushak, the man who sold it to me had brought it over in 1974."

**Defer.** The kitchen, forever. Paints the cabinets themselves in a tester pot of F&B Studio Green or a £4 Wilko dupe. The sofa is hand-me-down or £60 off Marketplace, covered in a Welsh blanket. Built-in storage is skipped in favour of freestanding Victorian wardrobes from Kempton, manhandled up the stairs of a Peckham conversion by two reluctant friends.

**Regret.** Almost nothing. Mistakes become stories ("that was the year I bought the broken pouffe in Margate"). Possible regret: a single fast-furniture purchase made under move-in pressure — a MADE side table, an IKEA Malm — which sits quietly resented.

**Decade-keep.** Everything. Most of it was already a decade old — or a century — when they got it.

**Crucial:** the flat is **never finished**, because it's accumulating. One more thing from a house clearance, one more lot won on The Saleroom. This is *not* the Maker's unfinished (pending projects) — this is *unfinished because a life is still being lived and narrated*. When they die, the belongings will be almost unsaleable — value was always relational, never material.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Storyteller vs Romantic.** Both collect old things. Romantic sorts by **decorative coherence** — everything reads pretty, soft, feminine: scallop, floral, sprig, a palette. Storyteller sorts by **narrative heterogeneity** — a taxidermy magpie next to a 1920s samovar next to a charity-shop Toby jug next to a signed Paul Nash. No palette. Romantic rearranges for the photo; Storyteller rearranges when a new inherited object arrives, following biography not colour.

**Storyteller vs Maker.** Both own a 1950s chest of drawers. Ask: Maker says "beech carcass, oak runners — I rebuilt the runners with beech dowels, stripped the shellac, waxed it." Storyteller says "that was my grandmother's in Pontypridd, she kept her sewing things in the top drawer, there's a 1962 receipt from Howells still inside." One reads the object as substance; the other as person.

**Storyteller vs Curator.** Curator curates editorially — a blue-label Ercol, a Robin Day chair, known by maker and mark. Rearranges for balance. Storyteller accumulates narratively: a 1970s Blackpool souvenir sits beside a William De Morgan tile and both are valued equally because both have a story. Curator knows the designer; Storyteller knows whose house it came from.

### Sensory anchors
The hallway smells immediately of **old books** — that slightly damp Penguin sweetness — and **beeswax polish** (Briwax dark, on the dresser). Underneath: Assam brewing strong in a brown Denby pot, a small undertone of Brasso rag in a jar under the sink, and in winter woodsmoke if the fireplace works. Sound: **Radio 4 is on, low** — *The Archers*, *In Our Time*, *Broadcasting House* on Sundays. A wind-up clock ticking, slightly out of time with itself. No ambient playlist. If there's music, it's a Nina Simone LP or Fairport Convention on a secondhand Rega. Shortbread from a tin, a ginger nut, tea so strong the spoon stands up. Light arrives through a cut-glass decanter, a geranium in a dented copper pot, three stacks of books — onto the kilim already patterned and broken. **Patinated brass, worn velvet, faded kilim, crackled glaze, foxed paper**, the papery dry-cool of an old Welsh blanket. The defining quality: a house with **many layers of smell accumulated over time** — none of them aggressive, none sprayed. A Minimalist would flinch. The Storyteller would tell you, accurately, that this is what home smells like.

## 7. The Urbanist · Urban Contemporary

### Behavioural tells
- Paints the bedroom ceiling Farrow & Ball **Preference Red** (or Paint & Paper Library **Euphorbia**, the designer-approved 2026 chartreuse) on a Sunday, having screenshotted it from a *Livingetc* piece on Tuesday and cross-referenced Wallpaper*'s colour trends 2026 round-up on Wednesday. Repaints within 18 months when the next cycle drops.
- Follows the **Dezeen / Wallpaper\* / Plaster** daily digest religiously. Forwards a Wallpaper* Design Awards link ("the Michael Anastassiades 'After' chair for Fritz Hansen… obsessed") on Slack at 9.17am.
- Books a four-top at **Brat, Ikoyi, Mountain, Lyle's or Mambow** for a flat-warming rather than cooking. The home is a *set*, not a kitchen.
- Takes the Elizabeth line or an Uber home from the Tom Dixon Shop at Coal Drops Yard, HAY on Rosebery Avenue, twentytwentyone, SCP Shoreditch, or the Gubi pop-up, with a single boxed object — a Melt pendant, a Beetle dining chair in Light Bouclé, a 5321 table lamp.
- Treats **London Design Festival** (September) and **Salone del Mobile Milan** (April) as personal pilgrimage. Takes the Wednesday off. Does Shoreditch Design Triangle, Brompton, Clerkenwell in one loop.
- Hacks the new-build kitchen: rips the developer gloss-white doors, orders Plykea Formica-faced fronts for the IKEA METOD carcasses in a directional colour with brass edge pulls. Tells everyone on Instagram Stories with a "the developer spec had to go" caption.
- Buys a Slawn print, a CIRCA edition, or a Saatchi Yates emerging artist. Sells last year's bouclé armchair on Vinterior or Gumtree to fund it.
- Swaps the sofa every 3 years. Repaints rooms annually around Easter bank holiday.

### Artefact grammar
**Present:** Gubi Beetle in bouclé or oxblood leather (a single one — £750+). A Serge Mouille three-arm ceiling fitting, or a Flos IC. An &Tradition Flowerpot or Topan. A Sabine Marcelis Candy Cube dupe in resin (or a splash mirror from RiRa Objects). A high-gloss lacquered sideboard with chrome legs. Handle-less sprayed Plykea-METOD kitchen, quartz or marble-effect laminate. A single oxblood leather sofa (SCP, MADE archive, La Redoute Interieurs bouclé) in an otherwise biscuit/chartreuse/off-white room. A drinks trolley (Gubi TS or Soho Home) with Del Maguey mezcal, Tutto natural wine, a Nick & Nora glass. A single large Slawn, Opake or Sam Bailey print *leaning, not hung*. The Ikoyi or Mountain cookbook on the counter, spine out.

**Scent:** Le Labo Santal 33, Byredo Gypsy Water, D.S. & Durga Debaser. Flowers: a single anthurium, red tulips cut short, or one sculptural stem — never a bunch.

**Absent:** visible cables. Jute. Open shelves piled with books. Exposed brick (Maker's territory). Gallery walls (Storyteller). Woven baskets (Nester). Any sense of apology.

### Language patterns
**Uses constantly:** "directional," "considered," "sharp," "tonal," "the colour is doing so much," "it's a statement," "we went quite bold," "feels current," "in conversation with [Gubi / Anastassiades / that Wallpaper\* cover]," "a bit of a moment," "a nice thing" (for the £1,200 pendant), "loud but considered," "the vibe." Will defend the painted ceiling. Never apologises.

**Excited about:** a Plaster Store takeover, a new Tom Dixon drop, London Design Festival, an SSENSE Home edit, the Tate Modern shop, a Hackney Wick warehouse studio show, a Slawn release, a Michael Anastassiades launch.

**Will not use:** *cosy* (almost insulting — Nester), *homely* (Nester), *quirky* (Storyteller), *timeless* (Curator — flags taste as reverential not present-tense), *pared back* (Minimalist), *pretty* (Romantic). Will never, ever call the flat cosy.

### First-time-buyer psychology
**Splurge:** one directional chair, £800–£1,800 (Gubi Beetle in Light Bouclé ~£1,500; HAY AAC in a new seasonal colour; &Tradition Little Petra). Always **new**, never vintage — newness is the point. A lighting piece that cost more than the sofa (Flos IC, Mouille repro, Original BTC). A sofa agonised over for six months on Instagram saves. Paint or wallpaper on a **feature ceiling** (the fifth-wall treatment, Wallpaper\* / Plaster 2026).

**Defer:** storage (hates visible storage; everything hidden, handle-less, or nothing). The full kitchen — waits, saves, Plykea-hacks rather than rip-and-replace. Daily flowers. Rugs (last to go in; must be considered).

**Regret:** last cycle's trend piece. The 2022 curly-bouclé everything. The terrazzo coffee table. The arched mirror everyone has. The sage-green kitchen from 2023. Hence the churn.

**Decade-keep:** 3–4 things only — the one directional chair, the Flos or Mouille, the signed Slawn/CIRCA print, possibly the sofa frame. Everything else is editorial; nothing is heirloom.

**Crucial velocity:** the Urbanist's flat is a **constantly-updated editorial set**. Annual repaints. Sofa every 3 years. Accent chair cycles faster. Sells on Vinterior with a confident product shot. This is the differentiator from the Curator, whose flat slow-builds toward a settled state over a decade.

### Adjacent-archetype differentiation
**Urbanist vs Curator.** Both design-literate. Curator: reverential and historical — buys vintage Ercol on Vinterior, quotes The Modern House journal, palette is oiled wood/paper/wool/plaster. Urbanist: directional and present-tense — buys the *new* Gubi Beetle Soft that launched this season, quotes Wallpaper\* 2026, palette is lacquer/chrome/oxblood/chartreuse. *Tell:* Curator apologises for the Söderhamn; Urbanist chose it in a deliberate colour with a Bemz cover.

**Urbanist vs Maker.** Both can read dark and hard-edged. Maker wants **visibly constructed** — exposed screws, dovetails, raw steel, welding seams. Urbanist wants **visually sharp** — seamless, handle-less, sprayed, no legible construction. *Tell:* Maker leaves the IKEA carcass raw-ply and puts a scaffold-board worktop on; Urbanist Plykea-faces it in high-gloss Formica with a brass edge pull and a sprayed return panel.

**Urbanist vs Minimalist.** Minimalist's restraint is **subtraction-as-quiet** — Vitsoe, Donald Judd references, everything away. Urbanist's restraint is **edit-as-statement** — one oxblood sofa in a biscuit room is a STATEMENT, not a quiet. *Tell:* Minimalist has no art on the wall; Urbanist has one giant Slawn and nothing else.

### Sensory anchors
Santal 33 on arrival, Gypsy Water in the bedroom, a Trudon or D.S. & Durga lit only when guests are coming. Light: dimmed, always. A single warm downlight on the one directional object. Philips Hue preset tuned to tungsten 2400K. No cold white anywhere, ever. Glossy surfaces pick it up. Sound: NTS Radio, Rinse FM, Boiler Room recordings, or a carefully-curated Discover Weekly on a Sonos Era, a Ruark MR1, or a B&O Beosound A9. **Never Radio 4.** A Pro-Ject Debut Carbon Evo with a coloured Ortofon cartridge — for show and for actual use. Touch: cold Caesarstone counter, chrome legs, bouclé on one upholstered piece, oxblood leather patinating, brushed steel trolley. A chilled Tutto or Noble Fine Liquor pét-nat in the fridge door; a half-bottle of Del Maguey Vida open on the trolley; no Nespresso pods — a Fellow Ode grinder next to a Moccamaster or Sage Dual Boiler.

| Urbanist — Nester | They almost never get confused — but note: both may own a slipcover sofa. Nester's is oatmeal linen and draped in throws; Urbanist's is a single ox-blood statement, unaccessorised. |

### Three behavioural axes that separate all seven

If the Barnum-defence ever slips, three axes reliably recover the distinction:

1. **Velocity of completion** — how fast the flat declares itself finished. Nester: 6 weeks. Romantic: ~1 year. Curator: ~5 years. Storyteller: never (accumulating). Minimalist: never (subtracting). Maker: never (building). Urbanist: annually overwritten.

2. **Object-count and display behaviour** — what's visible on a single sightline. Minimalist ~6 objects, Nester ~15 (but soft/matching), Curator ~30 (edited), Urbanist ~8 (one loud), Romantic ~40+ (pattern-stacked), Storyteller ~60+ (narrative-stacked), Maker ~20 (functional).

3. **What they apologise for vs what they defend** — the single most diagnostic language tell. Nester apologises for the kitchen, defends the candle spend. Curator apologises for the Söderhamn, defends the one Ercol. Minimalist apologises for bareness, defends emptiness. Romantic apologises for no pink walls yet, defends pattern density. Maker apologises for wood-filler, defends the half-finished project. Storyteller apologises for nothing, defends clutter as character. Urbanist apologises for nothing, defends the painted ceiling.

These three axes — *completion velocity, object-count, and the apologise/defend split* — are the Barnum-defence skeleton. Every archetype description should hit at least two of the three unmistakably.
